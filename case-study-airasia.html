case study

The team thought Browse was one launch and done. Research changed that.

Research that grew a roadmap and gave the next team a foundation to build it from.

A feature people liked, then lost, then kept asking for

Browse had existed before. Users liked it. Then a previous decision-maker took it down without a clearly documented reason, and the requests to bring it back never stopped.

The scale made this complex. More than 450,000 students across primary schools to junior colleges. Over 40 subjects, many offered across 2 to 3 curriculum bands and multiple year levels. The information architecture had to hold across all of that.

Meanwhile, curriculum specialists across more than 15 subject areas were building workarounds. Websites. Excel sheets. PDFs full of hyperlinks. Each one a different team solving the same problem the product hadn't.

These workarounds were the work of dozens of colleagues. That's a lot of effort to spend on something the product should have handled.

Role

Product Designer

Contributions

Research, Usability testing, Cross-division coordination, Problem reframing

Platform

The Singapore Student Learning Space (SLS), serving 500k users

My role in research, and mapping a dependency the team hadn't seen clearly

I ran usability testing and surveys with students, teachers, curriculum specialists, content map owners, and members of the original team who built the first Browse feature.

The research quickly surfaced something the product team hadn't fully mapped: content maps, owned by the curriculum division, were information architecture central to how Browse would need to work. I built out that picture proactively. It gave the team, including my product leads, enough shared understanding to start conversations with curriculum about updating them.

That meant joining curriculum team meetings, understanding how they were already tackling discoverability, and framing our work in terms that made sense to them. I also mapped overlap with adjacent platform features so we could coordinate rather than duplicate.

Strong advocates. But I discovered lukewarm reception.

A usability click map showed students tapping all over the screen without direction. Many couldn't find 'View all modules' at all. Seeing the pattern made this land with the team in a way a written finding wouldn't have, while powerfully changing our perspective of how children interacted with screens.

Placeholder: Screenshot of usability click map

Even among users who had explicitly asked for Browse to come back, testing reception was muted. The tree hierarchy made sense to students. The structure wasn't the problem. The presentation was. Students found it visually uninviting.

"People kept asking for Browse. Wow. I didn't expect it would be so lukewarm."

— Product Teammate

The module cards were a structural issue too. Limited information upfront meant students couldn't evaluate a module without clicking through. And a bigger question surfaced: even when Browse works, does it fit into how students actually revise? Discovery was a workflow problem that we hadn't anticipated due to avid advocacy, but my research surfaced.

The team used to think Browse was one launch and done

Before this research, shipping Browse was assumed to close the problem. My research findings led to alignment that more effort was needed. My product leads made room in the roadmap and product backlog made room for further iteration.

The next team carved out dedicated time for in-depth usability testing with students around revision habits, follow-up research that is rare on this team.

What I'd do differently

Starting with 2 sprints meant curriculum partners got looped in late, which slowed the metadata fix and meant my team struggled to have a deeper grasp of their content tagging workflow. I'd push for a realistic timeline before work starts, not after, so that we can unpack the underlying workflow for content developers more clearly. This would help us consider how to redesign this workflow too and stop problems upstream, supporting the browse catalogue's success.
